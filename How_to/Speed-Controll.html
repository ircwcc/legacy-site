How To Build  R/C Speed Controls
by
Marty Hayes










A historic look at speed controls in the hobby

Speed controls come in many sizes, shapes and prices. What is best for you is for you to decide. Items which should be included in your decision are such things as your skill, the size of your pocket book, availability of materials, the amount of current used by your motors and the size of the ship you are going to put it in. The choices run between the simplest toggle switch or brass contact type to the most expensive transistor electronic throttle.

In direct current (battery power), the motors turn in one direction when the polarity (+ and -) is applied to the contacts and the other direction when the polarity is reversed on the contacts. I have recently learned that motors are "tuned" to prefer one direction and will have a small power loss when running in the opposite direction (for most of our purposes, the loss is small, and can be ignored.) The motor reacts to the voltage applied in a direct manner, as more voltage is applied, the faster and more powerfully the motor turns. The two problems which will normally damage a motor are heat build up and too much current for the size of the wire which the motor is wound with. Both of these have to do with the application of too much voltage. The way to more power is a larger motor not more voltage past the limits of the motor windings. The amount of current drawn from your batteries and through your speed control is decided by the motors through their winding size and number. Cheap motors generally use more current (amperage) to produce the same power. Choose your speed control to carry the amount of current your motors require plus 50%.

How much control you need over your ship is again a matter between you and your ship. The speeds which you use in combat is somewhat dependent on your style of combat. Many people use very little other than full speed, some use many different speeds, some have run medium speed most of the time to conserve energy and have full speed to use in emergencies or for the chase. All ships must have a reverse by our rules, as a curb against rams. One of my old ships used a transistor speed control and has about 5 steps of forward speed. My own reverse was set at a lower voltage setting as full speed reverse tended to bury the stern (problems of a lower after deck.) In more recent years, I have used the MAG speed control with great success.

Simple Toggle Control

The one of the simplest speed controls is the toggle switch type. This is a simplistic design, requiring only a servo, the toggle switch (double pole, double throw, center position off), and a connecting rod. The wiring of the switch is such that the voltage is reversed as the switch is moved between it's extreme positions; the voltage is turned off in the center position. One of the problems with this design is, you only have full speed (forward/reverse) and off, no medium or slow speeds. Another problem which I have observed is the selection of the center position is sometimes difficult by radio control. All in all, this design is somewhat a bull in a china shop type of control. A few captains still use this type of control for secondary and cargo ships.

A similar design to the toggle switch uses a small slide switch with four positions giving two forward speeds and one reverse. This was described in an article by Mr. Oswald in Scale Ship Modeler which began in September 1981. This gave more control of the speed and became the hobby standard for a period of time.

Problems with this were occasionally the servo would over travel and hang the switch up at one end of it's travel which may or may not still be making contact and running the engines. The voltages for the slide switch throttle were pure (not using any resistors) but using different battery contacts to produce the different voltages required. This type is still used in combat occasionally.

Another similar design used a multiple position rotary switch (Radio Shackª) and operated either with resistors or different voltage pickups. The Rodney by Tom Jass used one of this design. The U.S. Quincy by the author used this type for it's first year of battle. The problems are similar to the previous designs in travel overruns and a limited choice of speeds. Advantages are cheap and easy construction, and a option which Tom uses. Tom uses the overrun feature of the servo trim to activate other controls (pumps in his case). This is accomplished by the use of a Radio Shack on/off switch which turns on when it is pushed once and turns off when it is pushed again. The switch is located so that the control arm contacts it only when the servo control is brought to the end of it's travel and the trim is brought to maximum also. Some captains also use the over travel of the rudder to accomplish this pump control.

Maryland Attack Group Speed Control




Top of Page


A new type of speed control has surfaced in the hobby (now in use for years) which is again of the forward, neutral, reverse type. This is the MAG (Maryland Attack Group) micro-switch type. Two micro-switches are mounted on a servo with a "half moon" cam such that neither are activated at neutral position. The arrangement was designed by my son-in-law Rich Schultz and myself one afternoon.  The switches are wired with the plus voltage on the N.O. position (normally open) and the minus (or negative) on the N.C. position. The motors are hooked up to the common positions such that one common goes to one side of the motor and the common of the other micro switch goes to the other. In the neutral position, the negative (minus) voltage is applied to both sides of the motor and the motor will not run. Activating either switches removes the negative and applies the positive to the motor contact connected causing the motor to run in that direction. This throttle is useful for ships consuming current only up to the limit which the micro switches can carry. Note: at neutral, the switches short out the motor contacts making it impossible to test the motors by attaching clip leads to them. Also this shorting of the motor contacts stop the motor quickly by shorting out the motor windings inductance build up.

Milholland Speed Control




Top of Page



The next speed control requires more construction skill and time, but seems to be a much more reliable design.  This is presented for historical purposes and is NOT recommended for your ship. The Milholland (M.S.C. Mark 1) is a resistive speed control (uses series resistors to drop the voltage across the motors.) The Drawing shows a rotary speed control which uses a brass wiper and brass contacts which have been epoxied to a wooden platform. The contact slides from plate to plate cutting out more and more resistance until at top speed the batteries are connected directly to the motors. The throttle has also be built in an in line configuration in which the plates are set in a row and the contact wiper arm slides up and down the plates. In the model shown, there are only three speeds forward and one reverse. You can make as many speeds forward as you wish by increasing the number of plates and allowing for more (but lower resistance) resistors. This throttle was described in greater detail in Hull Busters (Aug. '82). I have not seen this type in combat recently.

Resistive speed controls used in model race cars have and are being used. These are similar to the M.S.C. Mark 1 and only require you to hook up to your servo to operate. I might mention one problem with the resistive type is that they do waste that energy and create heat in the load resistors. Some people make a great deal of this fact but I don't think it is as great a concern. There are also some switch types of speed controls out for model race cars that can take a lot of current and don't drop energy in heating up resistors.

Transistor Speed Control
Top of Page

The final speed control is the deluxe transistor electronic speed control. The advantages are 1) they use no servo but operate directly from your receiver. 2) they offer a larger choice of speed range. 3) They are easy to install (in larger ships). The disadvantages are 1) they are expensive or complex to build. 2) they can take up a large amount of space, although newer ones are much smaller. 3) They don't care for water any more than the receiver and require waterproofing. 4) many if not most produce heat which must be disposed off. 5) To do it right, you should have a spare for anything in your boat and these are expensive to keep as a spare.  These are the top speed control but are used very little due to the cost and size. They can control from a slow speed in which the prop blades can be seen as they turn, up to a top speed (often bypassed with a relay) in which the motors receive full battery voltage with no loss of energy. There are no mechanical cams or rods to jam or over travel, and the units are very reliable unless they get wet.

Other Issues
One thing to consider is the use of fuses or circuit breakers in your boat. Fuses are devices which burn out at a specified current (amperage) and open the circuit to whatever device is on the other side of the connection. Some people use a fuse for each of the motors in their boat. If the motor jams up or is shorted out with a bb, the fuse is the item which fails saving a possible fire or enormous drain on your batteries. A single fuse on your battery pack will also work but if it burns out your ship will be left sitting on the lake with no power (this is not an advantageous position from which to do combat). Again the choice is yours but the use of fuses or circuit breakers offers advantages with very little weight. However, don't forget to put the fuses where they won't be shot.

Additional capacitors and coils can reduce the interference which your motors create. Each motor in your ship should have capacitors across the motor contacts and often one from each lead to the motor casing. The speed control can benefit from additional shielding and filtering. Twisted wires cut down on the EMI (electrical magnetic interference) as does shielded wiring (ground shield to the negative lead of the supply batteries.) Do not run your power leads for your motors close to your antenna lead in and be cautious in running high current flow wiring anywhere near your radio receiver and servo wiring. In extreme cases of interference, power the motors from relays (with diodes across the coil to prevent the spike which occurs when the coil discharges) located near the motors operated by switches in your radio box thus keeping the spikes far from your receiver. Speed controls should offer reliability above everything else, a sitting duck on the lake is a dead turkey. The second consideration is power drain as there is only a limited amount of power available. The third need is space and weight considerations within the size of your boat. The most important aspect of combat is the ability to move, followed closely by the ability to maneuver.
