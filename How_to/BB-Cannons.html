How To Build  R/C BB Cannons
by
Marty Hayes

The BB cannons or teeth of the ship (as Fluegel once called them) can be bought or made at home. BB cannons make R/C warship combat possible and, although 15 years of design evolution has made them simpler to build and operate, they continue to be the most complicated components used on-board ship.

Before learning how to build R/C cannons, you must learn how to operate them safely. Whenever operating a BB cannon, whether in the shop or on the water, you and the people around you need to wear shatter-proof safety goggles, with side shields. The cannons described here are very low powered, when compared to commercial air rifles and BB guns, but they can still damage a naked eye. Don't risk permanent eye damage: wear safety goggles whenever operating an R/C BB cannon.

The cannons consist of several distinct parts, as shown in the diagram:















The chamber or breech holds the BB and allows the pressure to build behind it until the BB slips past and is expelled with speed. A single shot selector which allows one BB and only one to be fed to the chamber. The magazine which holds the BBs to be fed into the single shot selector. A valve for turning the gas on and off, which can be a poppet valve or a solenoid valve operated by switches on your servos. Some kind of tank for your gas supply must also be stored in your ship. A regulator is used to make sure that the gas pressure used is within safe limits.

Breech Design


Mark IX Breech








The first type of shipboard BB cannon was designed and built by Stan Watkins in Texas in the early 1980s. Various models of this cannon were produced up to and through the famous Mark IX. The basic premise of this cannon was that an O-ring was first opened to a diameter greater than a BB and held in position by mechanical pressure. The pressure was slowly lightened and the O-ring slipped toward its original size. When the O-ring had slipped to the proper size and the BB was being retarded just enough to fire properly, the mechanical holding device was then tightened down to hold it in that position. This cannon was touchy, sometimes lost adjustment during the wrong time (was there a good time?), and required a certain touch (skill) to adjust properly. For all this cannon had against it, it launched a new hobby. R/C Model Warship Combat would have never started without it.

Restrictor Tube Breech


BACK







Because the Mark IX was so touchy to adjust, many people spent a good deal of time trying to come up with a better cannon. The next cannon to appear in the hobby 1984 created by Carl Camurati of New York by the GEEK cannon (so named because any geek could operate it). The chamber of this cannon is a piece of windshield washer hose or similar rubber hose which has an inside dimension slightly smaller than a BB. This performs the restricting part of the operation to allow the BB to stop and build up gas pressure behind it. There was no adjustment required after the initial installation and nothing to go out of adjustment. A piece of tape around the hose can calibrate this restricting method better. This disadvantage of the approach was that the tubing tends to wear out , rendering the cannon useless during a battle.

Modern versions of the geek cannon use two hoses, one inside the other, with an optional brass tube around the whole thing. This design is said to produce a more reliable breech, which doesn't have to be tuned.

Foster Breech



BACK






The present R/C combat standard is the Foster breech cannon (named for James Foster of Sparta, Mo., the inventor). This cannon works exactly the opposite of the Mark IX. It uses an O-ring with an ID just larger than a BB and the mechanical pressure adjustment forces the ID of the O-ring smaller thus restricting the BB and building pressure.

The Foster breech is generally built from standard 1/4" plumbing fittings, either using a straight-couple or a 90-degree elbow. The breech is formed by drilling out one side of the fitting so that it is slightly larger than the outer diameter of the O-ring, which is generally 5/16". This operation can be performed on either a lathe, milling machine, or a drill press. Care should be taken to make sure that the hole bored is concentric with the hole in the fitting itself.

For each Foster breech built, you must also build a corresponding barrel. Barrels are made out of 1/4" brass tubing, with a 5/16" flange at one end. The flange serves to press against the O-ring when a 1/4" compression nut is tightened around the barrel. That is, as the compression nut tightens, it pushes on the flange, which squeezes the O-ring. As the nut gets tighter, the O-ring is squeezed more and the cannon shoots harder. At some point, however, the O-ring will be squeezed so tight that it will no longer allow the BB to pass. At this point, the cannon must be tweaked to allow the BB to fire.

The Foster breech allows for a lot of experimentation and adjustment, including the size of the bore, the depth of the bore, the size of the flange and the adjustment mechanism. (Rick Shultz added a spring between the flange and compression nut to yield a self-tweaking breech.)

BB Selector

Guns used to fire in a fully automatic mode, where hundreds of BBs could be fired if the joystick were held down. Unfortunately, as gun reliability went up, this resulted in so much damage in such a short period of time that the hobby was not a very sporting event. Consequently, the IR/CWCC rules were modified to require all cannons to fire a single shot at a time, although some of the smaller warships can still fire "RIP" guns as the automatic ones are called. For most ships, only semi-automatic cannons are allowed. This means that for each complete operation of your transmitter control (on/off), the cannon would chamber and shoot only a single BB.

The design goal is to have some method of selecting one BB as the pressure is applied and firing a single BB. Here again, there are several methods of accomplishing this, using either a jam elbow, piston or ball bearing.

Jam Elbow Selector

BACK


The first method developed by Martin Schneider was the jam elbow in which the tubing between the magazine and the chamber was dead ended into a stub tube (the elbow) which held only one BB. As the pressure was applied the BBs were forced by the flow of gas against the stub. When the pressure was released the single BB fell into the line going to the chamber. The second application of gas pressure fired the first BB and repeated the above process leaving a new single BB selected and ready for chambering.

A modern jam elbow can be made simply from a 1/4" 90-degree elbow with a dimple drilled into the inside corner of the elbow. The dimple should be exactly 1/2 as deep as a BB. The elbow is mounted upside down in the boat, with both the magazine and the breech tube (ie. the tube leading to the breech) below the elbow. In this way, when the gas is applied through the magazine, a BB is jammed up against the dimple and it blocks all other BBs in the magazine. When the gas is released, the BB jammed into the dimple falls down into the tube leading to the breech, making it ready to fire the next time that gas is applied. At that point, the next BB will jam, preventing all BBs from going up the breech tube.

The jam elbow is simple to make and has no moving parts, thereby making it reliable. However, because it relies on gravity to chamber a BB, it cannot fire as fast as other selectors and it must be mounted properly in the boat. This latter requirement seems to be the most restrictive and the jam elbow has fallen out of favor.

Piston Selector




BACK




The next method developed was the piston selector created by Carl Camurati of New York. This used a piston at the end of the magazine to select a single BB and block additional BBs from being loaded.

The piston selector is generally built from a 1/4" tee compression fitting. The T-fitting is oriented so that the magazine and the breech tubing are at a 90-degree angle to each other, with the breech tube vertical and the magazine horizontal. Directly opposite and below the breech tube is the piston chamber, which forms the third part of the tee.

Pistons can be maded from a variety of materials, including brass stock turned on a lathe or stainless steel nails or rivets, cut to fit. Generally, anything that doesn't rust and that has a flat bottom can be used. The bottom of the T-fitting is bored out to allow the piston to ride up and down. The length of the piston is set so that the top of the piston is slightly below the magazine opening when no gas is applied, and so that it covers at least half of the opening when gas is applied.

The piston chamber is sealed using a standard 1/4" compression fitting which has a flat piece of brass soldered to the end. A small tube or barb fitting is soldered through that flat cap to allow the gas tube to be attached. When gas is applied, the gas pushes the piston up, blocking all the BBs in the magazine. Because the piston sits slightly below the magazine opening when no gas is applied, there is always one BB sitting on top of the piston, which is fired at the same time the piston is moving up.

Generally, a spring is placed around the piston itself, between the piston bottom and the top of the piston chamber. This spring pushes the piston down when the gas is turned off, decreasing the amount of time needed to chamber another BB on top of the piston. This allows for a very rapid rate of fire, restricted only by the time needed to chamber and fire a BB through the breech (which is determined by the O-ring pressure) and operator skill.

Sometimes, pistons are made in such a way that they completely seal off the piston chamber when in the up position. This seriously degrades the performance of the cannon, because there is not sufficient pressure in the breech to fire the BB properly. This problem can be overcome by using a pressure feed tube running from the piston chamber to the end of the magazine. This feed allows gas to flow down the magazine, through the piston chamber and up the breech tube, even while the piston is totally blocking the piston chamber from below. This also allows the bb's in the magazine to be pushed up toward the selector, ready for the next cycle of the piston.

The worst problem with the piston selector is the amount of skill or equipment needed to produce it. The reliability of this cannon is in the manufacturing and the resilience of the spring. Once a good combination is found, this type of selector can be reliable for a very long time, provided it is oiled regularly and the piston is deburred if necessary.

Ball Bearing Selector



BACK




Bob Amend of Pennsylvania, thought the piston was good but very hard to produce and came up with the poor man's piston cannon. By using a ball bearing as the piston he cut machining to a minimum and allowed the common man to produce a reliable weapon. The ball bearing chamber is built in basically the same manner as the piston chamber, except that the top of the chamber is rounded, instead of flat. This can be easily done with a standard drill bit. The ball bearing chamber is drilled so that when the ball bearing is pushed up by the gas, it covers at least half of the magazine hole.

BB Magazine


BACK




The magazine for these cannons are of basically two types, gravity feed and pressure feed. The gravity feed is arranged in the ship so the BBs constantly tilt toward the selector and move into the loading area under their own weight. The advantage of this method is that the magazine is not directly pressurized and the installation is easier. Also this is the easiest method for the ball bearing cannon. The pressure feed method uses the gas pressure which fires the cannon to also push the BBs toward the action. The last part of the magazine is almost always gravity fed in either method, tilting down at the selection fitting to allow the BBs to feed.

The most common magazines are made from 1/4" copper tubing, using either a straight piece or a piece that is wrapped in a circle or spiral. When bending copper tubing, care should be taken to bend it around a form to prevent it from crimping. Also, make sure that the bend isn't so tight that a BB jams in the spiral.

The end of the magazine that is attached to the selector is generally connected using a standard 1/4" compression nut or by soldering it directly to the selector. Care should be taken in the latter case not to flow solder into the selector itself, since that will jam the selector mechanism.


How the BBs are loaded into the magazine is also a source of planning and designing. Many people bring the loading cap above the decks of their ships to help in the turnaround time between sorties and make the job of loading easier. The magazines can also be contained below decks but I have found that reloading is more difficult in most of these below deck installations.

Tank and Regulator

All of our cannons presently operate using CO2. The CO2 liquid is kept on board the ships in commercially manufactured and certified tanks. Some people use disposable 2-ounce tanks, while others use 3.5-ounce, 7-ounce, or 10-ounce refillable tanks. Disposable tanks offer some convenience over refillable tanks for small ships, but the operating costs are roughly the same. CO2 tanks are generally purchased from stores or catalogs dealing in paintball equipment and can often be purchased as used equipment.

Since the ambient pressure of CO2 is over 800psi, a regulator is required to reduce the operating pressure to safe limits. The IR/CWCC rules limit the operating pressure to 150psi and further require that the regulator be purchased from a commercial manufacturer, without modifications. This is to ensure safe operation at all times.

Valves







The only section of the cannons which we need to discuss now is some method of turning them on and off. The most common method in the R/C world is presently a poppet valve and a mechanical link from your servo. A cam is produced by cutting down a servo wheel or attaching a cam to a servo by some other method. This cam pushes the poppet valve and releases the pressure to the cannon action. This method is inexpensive (<$10 per valve) and a single servo can operate two different valves. Poppet valves are also relatively compact and can be mounted directly through the sides of your radio box. Even on large battleships poppet valves can be used to operate multiple cannons by using two or more servos which operate off the same radio control lines.







A newer version of the valve control system uses a smaller (3-way) valve (at reduced pressure to operate the popets for the guns. This allows one servo to operate several guns (as in the case of multiple rear guns).


Another method for controlling the gas is the use of a cam on the servo which operates a switch which in turn operates a solenoid valve to control the cannons. Solenoid valves consist of an electromagnet and a spring. The spring keeps the valve normally closed, which is opened when voltage is applied to the electromagnet. Solenoids are generally more expensive than poppet valves ($35 each) and are generally heavier. However, because they are electrically actuated, they can be combined more easily and some people believe that they can be fired faster. The biggest advantage is that they can be located closer to the cannon itself, thereby decreasing the amount of gas needed to fire the cannon and the amount of gas tubing running throughout the ship.


Some captains installed fixed copper tubing throughout the ship to supply solenoids positioned near each cannon. Such an approach reduces the number of possible tubing leaks and provides a reserve of gas for each cannon. This prevents one cannon from robbing another of gas when they are fired together. Triple-fire cannons are almost always supported by small dump tanks on each gas feed leading to the solenoids. This provides each solenoid with a small reserve of gas which can be used to fire a cannon.

When using solenoids, care should be taken to reduce the amount of RF noise generated by them. When a solenoid is turned off, a rather pronounced burst of RF energy can be created by the electromagnetic coil. This burst should be stopped by using a diode soldered across the leads of the solenoid. To test the circuit, use an LED, which should light up momentarily when the solenoid switch is turned off. If it lights up when the solenoid switch is turned on, you have wired the diode backwards.

Mounting Cannons

The mounting of the cannons should be thought out ahead of time also. They should be mounted in such a manner as to be stable and yet adjustable. They can be mounted on the deck or to the hull and the deck put on around them. There are advantages and disadvantages to each method. The deck mounted cannons allow deck mounted magazines to be installed easier. These deck mounted magazines allow you to top off your BB supply easily while the ship is still assembled. Mounting the cannons to the hull allows a more solid mounting and less chance for crimping the feed hoses or creating leaks but fitting the decks around them in such a way as to make the cannons easily accessible tends to be a difficult feat at times.








The elevation of the cannons can be controlled by an additional servo or be fixed. No cannon can be elevated more than ten degrees above horizontal and all can be depressed to the amount allowed by their location on the ship, but no more than 20 degrees below the horizon. All cannons on a ship must be mounted in the main battery turrets except on those ships where these turrets are physically too small for the installation. In these few cases the cannons can be mounted in the superstructure in such a way as to not gain any advantages of fields of fire over what they would have if mounted in the turrets.

Where the cannons are mounted on your ship is a matter of choice to a great extent. On heavy cruisers and smaller ships you can only mount the cannons in either forward or aft pointing directions (up to 15 degrees off the center line is legal). On battleships the cannons can be mounted to the side (only one allowed per side) and in the forward and aft locations. Only three sides of a battleship can be protected by cannons under our present rules. This is to give the cruisers a chance against the BBs.

Reliability

As with everything else, reliability is important in the cannons. Nothing is more useless than a ship which won't fire (unless it's on the other side). The entire system must be free of gas leaks and the mechanical systems must be protected from damage and interference from other systems. Magazines made of copper tubing are susceptible to damage by cannon fire where a dent can jam the BBs and make the magazine useless. You can solve this problem by using brake tubing, brass tubing, or sleeving the copper with rubber tubing. Safety pins must be mounted on all cannon barrels when the cannons are not in use, and not removed until the ship is in the water or being tuned in a designated safe area.

Reliability is not something that can be designed into a cannon from the start: it takes hours and hours of experimentation, battling and re-working to yield a high quality cannon. You should expect to fire at least a few thousand rounds before understanding how the various parts of the cannon work together and to determine what works best for you. There is no single design that dominates any other and any cannon design can be made to work as good as another ON A GIVEN DAY. The best design is one that you understand very well and suits your battling and building style.
