How To Build  Superstructure
by
Marty Hayes














Superstructure can be made of anything your little heart desires due to changes in the original rules. Now the things to consider are weight and durability. Plastic has been suggested and was very much in use for several years on the larger ships. On the medium size ships such as heavy cruisers, the plastic construction may add too much weight topside and make an unstable ship. On the smaller ships heavy superstructure would capsize them.

In the early years all superstructure has been made of 1/32" balsa but the damage to it has at times been very bad, and it required replacement often. Since the damage to it has been almost incidental and contributed almost nothing to the scoring and unseaworthness of the ship, it was voted in 1984 or so to eliminate the unnecessary repair time and allow impenetrable superstructure. Over the years, the technology of superstructure has most certainly changed. I shall attempt to describe some of the old methods (which may still apply) and some of the newly used materials.

Deck House Construction

Most of our superstructure has been made of boxes in the past, and I see very little reason for this to go completely out of vogue in the future. The idea is to make boxes of each level, mount on the decks between levels and then glue the levels together. The different decks can be bolted, mounted on locater pins, or glued to each other and to the main deck. Note the boxes are not necessarily square on each side or corner; smoke stacks are generally rounded for example. With larger ships these boxes could be solid hardwood such as maple or pine (if you don't mind patching dents).


A section of deck which is raised above the main deck level can be constructed by cutting out a section of 1/16 or 1/8 plywood to the correct shape and setting it up on blocks to the correct level (approximately' 5/8" per level) and then hanging the vertical walls from it to the deck below. Difficult shapes such as smoke stacks can be constructed by a frame shape and covering or by building it solid and sanding it to shape and hollowing it out. Recently, the use of Lexan has come to be popular -- Hullbusters, in one of the spring issues of 1988 covered this medium.

I prefer a skeletal type of construction myself, in which a frame for the superstructure is made and then covered. This was not very difficult in the 1/32 balsa days and made replacement of the damaged skin relatively easy. This would still work with the plastic or thin plywood construction methods which are coming into use. The idea is to construct a skeleton of frames to form the shape and then cover the surface to fill out the form. For additional strength, the surfaces can then be coated with epoxy and the thin fiberglass cloth.

Fiberglass is coming into increasing use not only for hulls but also for major portions of the superstructure. Although probably not practical for a single ship, if your club or several of you decide to build the same class ship, you can produce a plug (single example of the superstructure section), a mold, and then copies for everyone.









Small Gun Construction
Detail items such as guns, railings and small life boats can be made or purchased dependent on your desires toward detail and your pocket book. These items can make a big difference in how your ship looks close up and some difference at a distance. Some of the ships in the fleet have really gone hog wild over detail such as the winner of one years best of scale at nationals. The Wisconsin by Jeff West of Florida even had the electrical piping and junction boxes on the outside of the superstructure.

I prefer making my own small guns although many of the small antiaircraft guns that are available have a good deal more detail than the ones which I am able to build. However, you would be surprised how good a few small pieces of wood and metal grouped together can look. The drawings of several small gun platforms which I have seen may give you an idea.








Railing Construction

Railings can be purchased from many of the scale ship equipment suppliers but they are not difficult to build and the home made version seems to be much more durable. The trick to good railings, guns, etc. is planning, design and where possible a building form. The drawing showing the railings and the form block to build them should give you the idea. To make the railing, first glue 1/16" balsa blocks in a pattern to hold the individual railings (small brass rods). I use a clamp made from spare tubing and a rubber band to hold the railings to the block. Now slip another individual piece of brass rod under the original ones at a 90 degree angle to form the vertical post. Solder the rails together using very little solder (carried by the iron) and a lot of flux (I use paste). Cooling is very quick and so clip off the excess rod and move to the next location and repeat the process. When you have attached several posts, move the whole assembly down and start again. When all posts have been installed, clean the flux off by scrubbing in the sink with an old toothbrush. I mount mine by drilling holes in the deck and super gluing them in the holes. These rails bend when hit by BB's but rarely break. My thanks to James West and his article in Hullbusters which started me on making my railing.








Small Ships, Boats, and Other Duplicate Items Construction
Ships boats can be built in various ways. Some people carve them out of solid blocks of wood and add the small details such as seats to them. Some create them by first making a frame and then covering them. Some start with the bottom and build sides, etc. like a small paper box you may have made in school. I purchase mine for awhile then made one which I then built a mold from for producing epoxy copies. The mold was built by vasolineing the plug to be molded and building up several layers of RTV.  The RTV mold is then peeled away and can be used to make more items of the same type.  Today there are types of mold producing rubbers available on the market such as the ones from Alumilite which also produces a epoxy like hardening material which Carl Camuriati of Fresh Meadows, N.Y. says holds up very well in combat.  Molds for other small items up to an including turrets can be built in the same method.

When making duplicate items such as turrets, 5 inch enclosed mounts, etc. I have sometimes found it easier to make up internal ribs and produce all of them at once. Another method which is gaining popularity but I have not tried is vacuforming these types of items. Vacuforming is done by first making a mold of the items you want to reproduce. This can be done by making one of each of the items. These are arranged in a vacuforming unit which heats a plastic sheet then sucks it down around the mold forming the pattern in plastic sheet. There have been several articles on making your own vacuforming kit from a vacuum cleaner and an oven in such publications as Scale Ship Modeler and Hull Busters. However, most of the grade schools in my area are into this and your youngster can perhaps do the job for you. Be warned that Vacuform shapes do not stand up the rigors of combat and will have to be armored and/or replaced.

Masts and Cross Trees Add a Lot
The masts and cross trees offer an easy way to make your ship stand out. The detail work on these items are perhaps the easiest to do and yet show up more and from farther away than any other item. The disadvantage is they are also the easiest to be tangled and broken. Adding the antenna wires and standing rigging is very easy and fast. I use the main masts and the antenna for my receiver antenna and gain extra distance on my receiver. My antenna wire is attached to the bottom of the mast as it sticks through the bottom of the deck by use of an electrical connector pin (one male and one female).

At times the superstructure has to serve other purposes than just be a pretty shape. Some operational guns are mounted in the upper turrets and needs support although it is located up in the superstructure. Magazine fills are sometimes located in the superstructure or hidden by it. Sometimes a moving piece can operate switches or unseen clamps to hold the removable deck on. Superstructure and it's effect on the operational model is best thought out ahead of time.

Superstructure construction today!

Closed cell foam and rubber is being used very often for superstructure in the present time. The kits from Swampworks contain a closed cell (not like a sponge) rubber which is cut to the shape of deck houses and absorbs battle damage (and bbs) well.  Others have used the Blue and Pink types of foam (House insulation found at most larger hardware stores) for deck houses and then either put an epoxy coating on or just put up with the bb damage.  David Lawrence of  Bel Air, MD uses Rustolium paint to cover his and says that two coats protects the foam well.
