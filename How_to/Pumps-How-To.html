How To Build Pumps
by
Marty Hayes










A historic view of pump construction

Bilge pumps are equipment which you may find handy to have around when your ship starts developing a rash of holes around it's water line. All pumps used in R/C combat must be of the impeller type (not positive displacement). The outlet hole in the hull for the pump discharge can not be over 1/8" inside diameter. The pump is only allowed one inlet and one outlet. The pump motor can not be any larger than your main drive motors. The standard hobby motor for pumps used to be a Dumas 4.8 volt electric motor. Now most people seem to use more powerful motors but the principles are still mostly the same. There have been various types of these pumps constructed and/or bought for the R/C combat ships and I shall try to give a brief description of each of them as an historic note. Then I will describe the standard form of a pump as used today in R/C combat.

Penny Pump







The first water pump produced in volume was the 'penny' pump described in Hull Busters a couple of years ago. The impeller for this pump was literally made of a penny with vertical fins soldered near the outer rim. A hole is drilled in the center of the penny and the penny is attached to the drive shaft of a motor. A housing is produced which is shaped similarly to the human ear. The impeller is mounted in the large round segment and forces the water into the narrow section where there is an opening to the water discharge. The housing is covered on the bottom except for a round hole (intake) over the center of the penny. The motor should turn in the correct direction to compress and move the water down the continually narrowing side of the housing.

The baby jar lid pump is similar except the housing (made from a baby food jar lid) is still round and a hole is made in it's side for the output. The mounting of the exit tubing is such that it's cross sectional area faces the water pressure built up by a swiftly turning impeller. Another improvement I have seen is the addition of another impeller blade on the same shaft which sits below the first impeller and scoops water up into the pump (this is also sometimes done by bending the bottom of the original blades and making them longer so they scoop water up into the pump.)

Foster Turbo Pump









The Foster turbo pump was by far the most powerful of all of the home made pumps. It's housing is made of wood or micarta¨ and it's impeller is made of several half sections of brass tubing mounted between two circular brass sheets. The completed impeller looks like a turbine blade hence the name. The impeller is mounted on the motor shaft with a dubro¨ collar, this allows the removal and adjustment of the impeller away from the housing. Most pumps in the hobby are of this type or a type similar to this now.

Another method once gaining acceptance is the automobile windshield washer pump. There were many who argue with this and some who swore by them. One problem with these is the motors which run them are made for intermittent duty and will burn up under continuous usage even when run with lower voltages than they would receive in an automobile. A second problem is many of them are of the positive displacement type - two or more small gears which pump the water due to very tight tolerances. The feeling is these can build up a high pressure pocket if their discharge is blocked, creating a possibility of an explosion, although I do not believe it. Therefore positive displacement pumps are not legal in the hobby. If the pump is an impeller type and if the motor is changed to a hobby motor type which can take continuous running under load, these pumps seem to be worthwhile for those who can't seem to build the other types and make them work. However, the pumps today pump between one and two gallons per. minute and the old windshield washer pump just doesn't do that.

Parts of a Pump








The most basic part of the pump is the impeller. This has been produced by the hobbiest in various ways. However, I will share with you my design for the impeller which has been serving me well over the last several years. I start with a 1 1/4 inch brass washer (available at most hardware stores). I then make a hub which fills in the large hole in the washer and provides a method of 1) attaching the blades and 2) attaching to the motor shaft. The hub is then "notched" with a hack saw for the number of blades I intend to mount (picture shows three notches), drilled and taped for a set screw and a motor shaft hole (normally near 1/8") is drilled through the center. All of this is easier on a lathe but most can be accomplished with out it. The blades are produced by taking a section of H&R brass tubing of the appropriate size, cutting lengths equal to the heigth of the blades, and then splitting the sections to form two blades. The impeller is then assembled by putting the hub into the center of the washer, the blades into the notches and setting on the washer. Flux everything and silver solder. I use relatively low temp silver solder although I know others who use the high temp stuff. I have found that I can use regular solder to wet the surface, then apply the silver solder to form an alloy. This seems to result in a stong enough joint (indeed I also used this method on model sailboat fittings which endure much more stress than the impeller.

The next item is the housing. This can be produced in wood, formica, etc. The housing must be waterproofed if it is a porious material! Basically it is a hole which the impeller fits with a top plate to hold the motor, a bottom plate to limit the intake to the center of the impeller, and an output tube which removes the water. To produce this item, you determine the height of the impeller and cut a block larger than the impeller in width and length, and slightly larger in height. Cut a round hole in the block again slightly larger than the impeller. Drill a hole into this open chamber for the output, this can be at an angle which tends to face the flow created by the impeller. Place a tube (often 1/4" copper or brass) into this, and bend upwards to direct the flow toward the exit (above the waterline of the ship). Now mount the motor to the top plate and the impeller to the motor shaft. By holding the plate to the top of the block align the impeller in the hole until it spins without touching the sides. Mount the plate to the block, and test for smoothness of the action. Now you can mount the bottom plate to focus the water flow. Next test the priming abilities of the pump by mounting the hoses and fittings which you will use in the ship. Lower into a pan or bucket until the pump is primed and pumping. Raise out of the water until the pump stops pumping. Now, again lower the pump into the water, if the pump does not imediately prime -- this is a priming problem. The problem is normally trapped water in the exit line which the air-bound impeller can not expel. One solution is to drill an extra small hole through the housing, so that the air can be expelled. This represents a loss to the system as water will be expelled through these holes after the pump is primed, but this loss is not expected to be great because they do not lower the pressure significantly. Another way of building the housing is to use existing shapes such as the 1" (ID) PVC fitting to form the housing and attach the outlet, motor, and bottom to that. As long as the general construction elements are in place, your pump should work.

Whatever type you build or buy, some other considerations are: 1/ screening off the intake of your pump to prevent it from ingesting something which it can't eat (a bb or some balsa wood fragments). 2/ Prevention of an air lock of the impeller. This is usually caused by leftover water in your exit lines which prevent the impeller from picking up prime due to the resistance in the output line. Something must be done to expell the air caught in the impeller. Corrections can include corrective positioning of the output lines so the water drains back into the pump housing (not a good solution) or drilling extra holes in the top of the pumping housing to let the air out. 3/ correct positioning of the pump(s) in your ship. A method of determining where the water will run to in your ship and putting a pump there, was described in an old Hull Busters by Mr. Flugel. In a nut shell, this was to determine the center of balance of your ship (on dry land) and the center of flotation (in the water), and place the pump between the two (nearer the center of floatation is best). 4/ some method of turning the pump(s) on. You can leave on all the time (an energy waste but limited since the current drawn by a motor which is not under load is low), turn on by servo control, or make an automatic switch (either mechanical or electrical). The mechanical float type was not very successful to this point in the R/C fleet, but the Texas group have been reviving it with good success. 5/ where on the ship you intend to exit the pump water. A majority of the R/C modelers send it up into the air (which makes a neat sight and an ineffective weapon {except against Transmitters}), some eject it out to the side. and some aft to provide some propulsion. The choice is yours, based on how much you wish to advertise the fact that you are damaged (to yourself and others).

A pump can be your ships best friend at times but at other times an enemy. The time it is an enemy is when it is just delaying the inevitable and gaining you extra damage when you are going to sink anyway. At a time like this, you don't need friends like a running pump. Some of the combatants have not only designed methods of turning off their pumps but even (I am told) have gone as far as to add some method of scuttling their ship. Reliability and control are again important in your pump and it's control systems as with all the systems in your ship.

In more recent times, a good commercial bilge pump has become available. Contact Swampworks or Battle Connections (ads in most model boating magazines and on the commercial links page) for price and details on commercial bilge pumps.
