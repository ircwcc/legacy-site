Hull Construction
by
Marty Hayes
A historic view of hull construction in R/C combat









Normally the first thing to be constructed is the hull, although it is not necessary to start with it. The hull can be constructed in various ways as long as it meets the requirements of the combat rules. The main things to keep in mind are 1) ribs can be no thicker than 3/8 inch material. 2) from 1 inch below the water line to the top of the hull, all horizontal skin sections must be made of 1/32" balsa. 3) Minimum spacing of ribs shall be no less than 1 inch. 4) Solid material in the bow can extend no more than 2 inch. aft. and in the stern no more than 1 inch. forward. 5) The total hull length which can be solid material can not exceed 15% of the total length of the hull. This includes ribs, and solid material at both ends of the ship. 6) Hulls can not be compartmented and can have no waterproof bulkheads.
Wooden Hulls

As long as the points above are met, almost any type of hull construction method can be used. The most common type is shown below; this is a modification of the style used in the original Texas plan sets. The ribs are attached to a central keel and the ship is built from the keel up. The bottom can be built of any material up to 1 inch. below the water line. Solid balsa, plywood, fiberglass, 1/16 inch. balsa covered with fiberglass or model aircraft skin and plank on frame construction have all been used. The top frame of 1/8 - 3/8 inch. plywood is then installed on top of the ribs to form the deck. Advantages of the keel up method is that the keel is easily kept straight by attaching it to a building board.








Top down construction is also used quite often. In this method the cap deck (deck on top of the ribs) is laid out and the top of the ribs attached to it. Formers are attached to the bottom of the ribs and/or a keel can be attached. The advantage of this method is that of a straight deck. Step decks can be accommodated by making a building board with a step. I tend to use this method for smaller ships. Both of the above methods produce basically the same type of hull made from same materials.

A modified plank on frame, solid plywood bottom, solid balsa bottom, and wood reinforced fiberglass have all been used for the bottom of ships. Remember these 'hard' surfaces must stop one inch below the waterline. However each of the above method produces a stronger boat than one built of just frames. keel and skin. Also most of these methods weigh more than a straight balsa skin boat. Another consideration in "solid" bottom boats is sinkability; our rules state each ship must be able to sink, be aware that too much solid wood plus the addition of a waterproof box for the radio and controls may produce an unsinkable and therefore illegal ship. This has happened but mainly in the cases of cargo ships which have limited equipment on board.

Today, most combatants are concerned with water flow and now build ships which have two keels spaced apart down the length of the ship to allow water to flow to the pump(s). The outside of these keels are filled in with 1/4" thick sheets (often balsa) so that any water taken on flows to the channel. The bow and the stern are built in the normal fashion but the channel is set up through the middle portion of the ship.








Scaling the plans and transferring the patterns to wood are not as difficult as they may seem. First we will take up scaling the plans to our scale (1"=12'). The easiest and most expensive method is to take them to a print shop and have them reproduced in the proper scale either by photo enlargement or their large 'xerox' machines. Another method is to use the old squares vs. squares method which you may have learned in grade school. I produce a set of small squares on a translucent material and position this over the original set of plans. I then draw a set of squares of the proper ratio on another sheet of paper. Transfer of the shape and size is accomplished by matching the intersections of the plans with the positions within the squares. An article on converting scales appeared in the magazine, Model Ship Builder , July/August 1986.

Another system which has been mentioned in Hull Busters is to take a photograph (slide) and project it on a sheet of paper (on the wall) in the proper size. Trace the outline of the ribs, keel, etc. on the paper. Care should be taken that the slide does not heat up and change slightly during this process. An addition to this method, which I read about the other day is to add a grid over the original to check distortion as you go through the process.









Lofting the plans is another method of changing the scale which was used by the ship builders. To use this method, you need a lot of paper. Basically, you place the rib sets in the lower left quadrant of a large sheet of paper and draw lines in the above quadrant and the lower right quadrant which have the slope which corresponds to the scaling change you want to make. Now, lines brought up to the line and brought over the the top right quadrant meet with lines brought to the right and up to form the points for the new scaled drawing representing the original drawing scaled up or down.

Thanks to modern machinery, almost everyone has access to a coping machine with scaling features. The scaling feature of these machines today make scaling easy although it may be necessary to do them in multiple steps. However, the methods in this article predated easy access to coping and scaling machines, and large size drawings such as full view side and top shots may be too expensive to copy.

If you buy plans in the proper scale, the first problem is bypassed. There are various methods for transfer to wood. When I built my first ship, I used carbon paper under the plans for transferring the ribs and keel to wood. The method works but I think there are better ones to use. If you xerox the rib outlines and then iron the back of the xerox sheet, the ink in the paper is reactivated and transfers to the wood. This takes a bit of heat and some time but you'll get better as you practice. Another time I cut out patterns and traced around them or glue them to the wood with rubber cement. By xeroxing the plans into overhead transparencies you can display them on the wood or the wall and trace or if you make many of them you can cut them into patterns and trace around them. Many books seem to give you half hull sections but not to worry, you can produce the other half by xerox of the transparency (which can flip over to produce the other side of the ribs). Another way is to trace the first half in heavy pencil lines and fold the paper at the centerline and rub, the carbon will rub the outline to the opposing side. Don't forget to subtract the thickness of the deck as you trace the ribs. The plan set may or may not have taken this into consideration. The thickness of the bottom if it is greater than 1/32 inch. should be considered also.

Deck Installation

The method of attaching the upper deck to the top of the ribs is also a matter of choice. I prefer to attach what I call a cap deck on top of the ribs made of 1/8" plywood (see illustration on pg.14A). This cap deck simply ties the ribs together and gives the deck form. The inside of this deck is cut off close to the inner dimensions of the ribs. A second 1/8" sheet is attached on top of the cap deck. This is the real deck. Access holes are cut through the top deck in such a way as to have the cap deck support them. Scraps from the cap deck or tabs are glued to the bottom of the main deck to support the access panels by forming ledges, preventing the access hole covers from falling in. These covers can be attached in various ways such as screws, friction, clamps, or lockdowns of various types. Screws are the least desirable from the standpoint of removal of the deck for servicing, and friction is the least desirable from the standpoint of security (such as when sinking.) In some cases topside equipment such as secondary guns, lifeboats etc. have been used to hide or be handles for holddown clamps.

Today many combatants have a full removable deck with just a 1/8" to 1/4" rim around the top of the cap deck to hold it in position. Often the guns are attached to this deck and are removed with the deck for servicing.










Once the frame has been constructed and is ready for skinning it will be difficult to keep from continuing on and putting on the 1/32" skin. But .... in general, it is better to save this for the very last. First off it is easier to get to everything in the ship with the skin off. Secondly the skin is somewhat fragile and you will damage it several times as you work on other parts of the ship if you install it early. However, think as you install equipment of how you will service it once the skin is on.

You should attach the deck, cap deck, keel, and frames together making sure everything is straight and true. A building board helps you to accomplish this. Draw a straight line down a flat piece of 3/4" pine shelving and line the keel up on the line. Smooth the ships lines outside of the frames by sanding between two or more frames to minimize the amount of crimping of the skin which is being done at each frame. Deck lockdown systems should be installed at this time and made as easy and reliable as possible. Prop shafts can be made and installed at this point or installed later; I prefer now.

In many recent ships the interior of the hull has been made as flat as possible by various methods. One method is to pour fiberglass  resin (made lighter with saw dust or micro-balloons into the interior of the hull. The hull can be tilted so the fiberglass ¨ forms an angle that leads the water to the pumps.  Longitudinal strips can be put down in the center to keep the fill to the sides forming a channel. The ribs are then cut down even or below the level of the fiberglass¨. Another method is to attach plywood onto the bottom of the hull and then cut out th middle of all of the ribs (and fiberglass).  The advantage of these is the interior is as clean as possible and there is little to interfere with the installation of the ships systems. A second advantage is the water going to the pumps has very little obstruction. Simply cutting down the width of the ribs as they cross the bottom and providing large drainage holes for water through the ribs accomplishes this in smaller ships which can not afford the weight of the heavier handed methods. A newer method is to provide a channel through the center of the ship to lead the water to the pump, this is used in those ships which have thick bottoms.  The sides are built up using balsa blocks or foam and covered with a epoxy coat.

The position of everything in the ship should be planned at this time and provisions for them installed. Such things as platforms and brackets for pumps, motors, waterproof boxes, and batteries are integrated into the hull more easily now than after the skin is attached.










Making the deck fit on to the hull in the above manner makes a snug and seaworthy vessel. However, access to the interior of the hull is limited and the interior systems are not as easily serviced. The use of deck houses to hide the openings work well and the interior walls limit "splash" water from entering the boat easily. BB fill tubes and other equipment are readily accessible when the decks are removed, and no extra hold downs are required. I would not recommend this type for your first ship but it could be used as the ships' systems become more dependable and require less maintenance in the field.

Fiberglass Hulls

Now Fiberglass hulls are available for many of the ships used in combat (from our combat suppliers) and the fiberglass hulls have become rule rather than the exception.  Since the ship's shape has been produced, the job of building the ships has become much easier.  First thing is to provide some method of attaching the deck and to strengthen the top of the hull.  This is usually done by epoxying strips of wood inside of the top edge of the hull.  This can be done in several ways: 1) cut out a sheet of plywood to fit just inside the hull edge or 2) line the sides with strips of wood (often thin ones built up on top of each other).  The hull might need some cross members to help it hold its width over time, these can also be epoxied in at this time.

One real advantage of the fiberglass hulls over the built up wooden ones is that they are watertight NOW!  This means that the placement of the equipment can be tried out inside the hull and floated to determine where everything goes and how the ship sits in the water.  Do this and mark where everything will be in the hull.  Be mindful of the depth of guns (such as over water proof boxes and batteries).

The Hull will also need to be built up inside to make the water flow channels to the pump.  This is easily accomplished by use of balsa blocks or craft foam (rubber or plastic available at craft shops) which is glued down to the inside of the hull.  Thought at this point can produce compartments for batteries, radio box and pump which keep the equipment low and from shifting around and still allows clear and easy flow of water to the pump.

Back to the top of the hull.  The plywood insert or wood strips have now strengthened the top edge of the hull and formed a "subdeck" on which the real deck will sit.  The deck can overlap the outside of the hull (and be sanded even to it) or can stop somewhere on the subdeck where mounted wooden strips or plywood complete the journey to the outside of the hull.  That is to say that the sub deck and some wood glued on top of it forms a edge around the outside of the top of the hull which keeps the deck in place.  See "Deck Installation" in the wooden hull section.
